The Real business cycle model

The so called Real busiless cycle model (RBC) was introduced in 1982 by Kydland and Prescott [1] building on a discrete-time version of the neoclassical growth model framework. Crucially however, uncertainty is introduced in the form of exhogenous productivity shocks, so that productivity follows the \(AR(1)\) process \begin{equation} \label{eq:productivity_process} z_{t+1} = \varphi z_{t} +\varepsilon_{t+1}. \end{equation} Moreover, a choice between the fraction of time devoted to labour \(l_t\) and leisure \(h_t\) is also introduced with the natural constraint \begin{equation} l_t+h_t = 1. \end{equation} We consider a Cobb-Douglas per-capita production function \begin{equation} f(k_t, l_t, z_t) = z_t k_t^\alpha l_t^{1-\alpha}, \end{equation} with as usual \(\alpha\) being the output elasticity of capital. Because of the stochasticity of the productivity process \eqref{eq:productivity_process} consumers will aim at maximising expected utility, so that the optimisation problem reads \begin{equation} \label{eq:rbc_opt_problem} \boxed{ \begin{split} &\max_{c_t, h_t}\mathbb{E}\left[\sum_t\frac{1}{(1+\rho)^t}\left((1-\eta)u(c_t)+\eta\nu(h_t)\right)\right]\\ &\phantom{xxx}\text{subject to}\\ &k_{t+1} = l_t w_t + (1+r_t)k_t - c_t \end{split} } \end{equation} Notice that the household has two control variables: consumption \(c_t\) and leisure \(h_t\). Here \(0 < \eta < 1\) is a mixing parameter determining the relative relevance of consumption utility versus leisure utility in the total utility function ...
Problem \eqref{eq:rbc_opt_problem} has Bellman equation \begin{equation} V(k_t) = \max_{}\left\{(1-\eta)u(c_t)+\phi\nu(h_t)+\frac{1}{1+\rho}\mathbb{E}\left[V(k_{t+1})\right]\right\} \end{equation} which is maximised for \begin{equation} \label{eq:opt_rbc_bellman} \frac{d V(k_t)}{d c_t} = (1-\eta)u'(c_t)-\frac{1}{1+\rho} \mathbb{E}[V'(k_{t+1})] = 0, \end{equation} that is \(\frac{1}{1+\rho} \mathbb{E}[V'(k_{t+1})]=(1-\eta)u'(c_t)\). Then, notice that \begin{equation} \frac{d V(k_t)}{d k_t} = \frac{1}{1+\rho}\mathbb{E}[V'(k_{t+1})](1+r_t) = (1-\eta)u'(c_t)(1+r_t), \end{equation} so that \(V'(k_{t+1}) = (1-\eta)u'(c_{t+1})(1+r_{t+1})\). Therefore from \eqref{eq:opt_rbc_bellman} one has \begin{equation} \begin{split} u'(c_t) &= \frac{1}{1+\rho}\frac{\mathbb{E}[V'(k_{t+1})]}{1-\eta}\\ &=\frac{1}{1+\rho}\frac{1}{1-\eta}\mathbb{E}[(1-\eta)u'(c_{t+1})(1+r_{t+1})]\\ &=\frac{1+r_{t+1}}{1+\rho}\mathbb{E}[u'(c_{t+1})] \end{split} \end{equation} that is to say, the Euler equation is: \begin{equation} u'(c_t) =\frac{1+r_{t+1}}{1+\rho}\mathbb{E}[u'(c_{t+1})]. \end{equation}
