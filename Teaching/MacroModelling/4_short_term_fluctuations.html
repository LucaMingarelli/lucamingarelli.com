Short Term Fluctuations

The previous chapter has focused on long-term dynamics and the process of capital accumulation. One is however often interested in the short-term dynamics of business cycles, that is fluctuations in output and employment around a long-term trend.
Some stylised facts characterising the expansions and contractions of the business cycle are:
  1. Fluctuations are recurrent but with no trivial cyclical pattern, and hence hard to predict
  2. Persistence of both expansions and contractions through time
  3. Employment and consumptions are procyclical, that is they co-move with output
  4. Real wages exhibit little fluctuation with and are only weakly procyclical if at all

The Real Business Cycle model

The so called Real busiless cycle model (RBC) was introduced in 1982 by Kydland and Prescott [2] building on a discrete-time version of the neoclassical growth model framework. Crucially however, uncertainty is introduced in the form of exhogenous productivity shocks, so that productivity follows the \(AR(1)\) process \begin{equation} \label{eq:productivity_process} z_{t+1} = \varphi z_{t} +\varepsilon_{t+1}. \end{equation} Moreover, a choice between the fraction of time devoted to labour \(l_t\) and leisure \(h_t\) is also introduced with the natural constraint \(l_t+h_t = 1\). We consider a Cobb-Douglas per-capita production function \begin{equation} f(k_t, l_t, z_t) = z_t k_t^\alpha l_t^{1-\alpha}, \end{equation} with as usual \(\alpha\) being the output elasticity of capital. Because of the stochasticity of the productivity process \eqref{eq:productivity_process} consumers will aim at maximising expected utility, so that the optimisation problem reads \begin{equation} \label{eq:rbc_opt_problem} \boxed{ \begin{split} &\max_{c_t, h_t}\mathbb{E}\left[\sum_t\frac{1}{(1+\rho)^t}\left((1-\eta)u(c_t)+\eta\nu(h_t)\right)\right]\\ &\phantom{xxx}\text{subject to}\\ &k_{t+1} = l_t w_t + (1+r_t)k_t - c_t \end{split} } \end{equation} Notice that the household has two control variables: consumption \(c_t\) and leisure \(h_t\). Here \(0 < \eta < 1\) is a mixing parameter determining the relative relevance of consumption utility versus leisure utility in the total utility function ...
Problem \eqref{eq:rbc_opt_problem} has Bellman equation \begin{equation} V(k_t) = \max_{}\left\{(1-\eta)u(c_t)+\phi\nu(h_t)+\frac{1}{1+\rho}\mathbb{E}\left[V(k_{t+1})\right]\right\} \end{equation} which is maximised for \begin{equation} \label{eq:opt_rbc_bellman} \frac{d V(k_t)}{d c_t} = (1-\eta)u'(c_t)-\frac{1}{1+\rho} \mathbb{E}[V'(k_{t+1})] = 0, \end{equation} that is \(\frac{1}{1+\rho} \mathbb{E}[V'(k_{t+1})]=(1-\eta)u'(c_t)\). Then, notice that \begin{equation} \label{eq:V_prime} \frac{d V(k_t)}{d k_t} = \frac{1}{1+\rho}\mathbb{E}[V'(k_{t+1})](1+r_t) = (1-\eta)u'(c_t)(1+r_t), \end{equation} so that \(V'(k_{t+1}) = (1-\eta)u'(c_{t+1})(1+r_{t+1})\). Therefore from \eqref{eq:opt_rbc_bellman} one has \begin{equation} \begin{split} u'(c_t) &= \frac{1}{1+\rho}\frac{\mathbb{E}[V'(k_{t+1})]}{1-\eta}\\ &=\frac{1}{1+\rho}\frac{1}{1-\eta}\mathbb{E}[(1-\eta)u'(c_{t+1})(1+r_{t+1})]\\ &=\frac{1+r_{t+1}}{1+\rho}\mathbb{E}[u'(c_{t+1})] \end{split}\quad . \end{equation} Similarly, for labour-leisure choice one has \begin{equation} \label{eq:opt_rbc_bellman2} \frac{d V(k_t)}{d h_t} = \eta\nu'(h_t)-\frac{1}{1+\rho}\mathbb{E}[V'(k_{t+1})]w_t = 0, \end{equation} meaning that \(\frac{1}{1+\rho}\mathbb{E}[V'(k_{t+1})] = \eta\nu'(h_t)\). Therefore, putting this result together with \eqref{eq:V_prime}: \begin{equation} V'(k_{t+1}) = \frac{\eta\nu'(h_{t+1})}{w_{t+1}} (1+r_{t+1}) \end{equation} and \begin{equation} \begin{split} \nu'(h_t) &= \frac{w_t}{\eta(1+\rho)}\mathbb{E}[V'(k_{t+1})]\\ &= \frac{1+r_{t+1}}{1+\rho}\mathbb{E}[\frac{w_t}{w_{t+1}}\nu'(h_{t+1})] \end{split}\quad. \end{equation} That is to say, the Euler equations are: \begin{equation} \label{eq:rbc_euler_equations} \begin{split} u'(c_t) &= \frac{1+r_{t+1}}{1+\rho}\mathbb{E}[u'(c_{t+1})],\\ \nu'(h_t) &= \frac{1+r_{t+1}}{1+\rho}\mathbb{E}[\frac{w_t}{w_{t+1}}\nu'(h_{t+1})]. \end{split} \end{equation}
In order to clarify the meaning of this result it is useful to look at the deterministic case with log-utility. In this case, equations \eqref{eq:rbc_euler_equations} become \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{c_{t+1}}{c_{t}} &= \frac{1+r_{t+1}}{1+\rho},\\ \frac{h_{t+1}}{h_{t}} &= \frac{1+r_{t+1}}{1+\rho}\frac{w_t}{w_{t+1}}. \end{split} \end{equation} Therefore, higher wages increase the supply of labour proportionally to the level of interest rates.
