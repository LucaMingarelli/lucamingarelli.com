Macro-Economic Modelling 1

Growth Theory

In 1957, Nicolas Kaldor stated[1] some empirical facts that economic growth seemed to satisfy. These became known as the Kaldor facts:
  • Output per worker growth continuously
  • The ratio of capital to output is approximately constant
  • Capital per worker grows continuously
  • The rate of return on capital is approximately constant
  • Labour and capital receive contant shares of total income
  • The growth rate of output per worker is vastly heterogeneous across countries

The Solow model

Let us consider an economy with only physical capital \(K\) and labour \(L\) as inputs, producing output \(Y\) according to the production function \begin{equation} Y = F(K, L, t), \end{equation} which is assumed to have the following properties:
  • Positive and diminishing marginal products with respect to each input, that is \(\partial_x F>0\), \(\partial_x^2 F < 0\), for \(x \in \{K,L\}\);
  • Constant return to scale, that is \(F(\lambda K, \lambda L) = \lambda F(K, L)\) for any \(\lambda >0\);
  • Respect the following Inada conditions \begin{equation*} \begin{split} &\lim_{K\rightarrow 0}\partial_K F = \lim_{L\rightarrow 0}\partial_L F = \infty\\ &\lim_{K\rightarrow\infty}\partial_K F = \lim_{L\rightarrow\infty}\partial_L F = 0\\ \end{split} \end{equation*}
Such a production function is referred to as a neoclassical production function.
In such an economy, output is a good which can either be consumed or invested to create new capital. Denoting by \(s\) the fraction of output saved (saving rate), and by \(\delta>0\) the constant depreciation rate of capital, one can write the following equation of motion for the amount of capital in the economy \begin{equation} \dot{K} = s\cdot F(K,L,t) - \delta K. \end{equation} Notice that the constant return to scale condition assumed for the production function implies that \(F(K,L) = L\cdot F(K/L, 1)\). For simplicity, let us therefore define the output-per-capita \(y = Y/L\), capital-to-labour ratio \(k=K/L\), and \(f(k):=F(k,1)\). The equation of motion for capital can therefore be rewritten as \begin{equation} \frac{\dot{K}}{L} = s\cdot f(k,t) - \delta k. \end{equation} Further assuming a constant exogenous growth rate of labour (assumed equivalent to the population) of \( n = \dot{L}/L > 0\) (that is \(L(t) = e^{nt}\)) we can further find \(\dot{K}/L=\dot{k}+nk\), so that the equation of motion finally turns into \begin{equation} \boxed{\dot{k} = s\cdot f(k,t) - (n+\delta) k.} \end{equation}
