Macro-Economic Modelling 2

While insightful and useful to analyse a large spectrum of macroeconomic issue, the neoclassical growth model looked at in the previous section is still based on the assumption of identical and infinitely-lived agents. This prevents the study and analysis of interactions between cohorts at different stages of their lifes, which may be important in evaluating policies around pensions, taxation, demographics, etc..

Overlapping Generation Models

The Samuelson-Diamond model

Let us start by considering a discrete-time model with two overlapping cohorts due to Samuelson [1] and Diamond [2]. The economy cosists of individuals and firm. Individuals live for two periods: young-age and old-age. In the former period they work for firms and receive a wage \(w_t\), and lend their savings \(s_t\) to firms for which they are remunerated at a given rental rate \(r_t\); in the latter period they retire. The population grows as before as \(L_t=L_0(1+n)^t\). Firm on the other hand are assumed to be competitive and use produce according to the production function \(Y=F(K,L)\). \begin{equation} U(c_{\text{young}}, c_{\text{old}}) = \frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}c^{\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}}_{\text{young}, t}+\frac{1}{1+\rho}\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}c^{\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}}_{\text{old}, t+1} \end{equation} The maximisation problem for an individual born at time \(t\) is: \begin{equation} \boxed{ \begin{split} &\max{U(c_{\text{young}}, c_{\text{old}})}\\ &\phantom{xxx} \text{subject to}\\ &c_{\text{young}, t} = w_t-s_t\\ &c_{\text{old}, t} = (1+r_{t+1})s_t\\ \end{split} } \end{equation} Firms produce competitively, maximising profits \(\Pi_t=F(K_t,L_t)-r_tK_t-w_tL_t\). That is, it produces renting capital up to when the marginal product of capital becomes equal to its rental rate (\(\partial_K\Pi=0\)), and hiring labour up to when then marginal product of labour equals the wage (\(\partial_L\Pi=0\)): \begin{equation} \boxed{ \begin{split} \frac{\partial F(K, L)}{\partial K}&=f'(k_t) = r_t\\ \frac{\partial F(K, L)}{\partial L}&=f(k_t)-k_tf'(k_t) = w_t \end{split} } \end{equation} where as before quantities are expressed in per-capita terms.
Finally, market equilibrium requires net investments (left) to equal net savings (right): \begin{equation} \boxed{ K_{t+1}-K_t = L_t s_t - K_t. } \end{equation}

Let us start by solving the individuals maximisation problem.
First, notice that \begin{equation} \begin{split} \partial_s U &= c^{\frac{-1}{\sigma}}_{\text{young}, t} \cdot \partial_s c_{\text{young}, t}+\frac{1}{1+\rho}c^{\frac{-1}{\sigma}}_{\text{old}, t+1}\cdot \partial_s c_{\text{old}, t}\\ &= -c^{\frac{-1}{\sigma}}_{\text{young}, t}+\frac{1+r_{t+1}}{1+\rho}c^{\frac{-1}{\sigma}}_{\text{old}, t}. \end{split} \end{equation} Therefore, the first-order condition for maximising \(U\) with respect to \(s_t\) is \begin{equation} \label{eq:sd_foc} \frac{c_{\text{old}, t}}{c_{\text{young}, t}} = \left(\frac{1+r_{t+1}}{1+\rho}\right)^\sigma. \end{equation} It is also easy to check that \(\partial^2_s U < 0\), so that \eqref{eq:sd_foc} provides indeed a sufficient condition for a maximum.
